\begin{frame}[c]{What are intermediate results, early metrics or proxy performance?}

Evaluating ML systems is often iterative and we can get intermediate results such as:
\begin{itemize}
    \item subsample dataset
    \item partial training
    \item coarse simulation / downscaled images
    \item smaller model
\end{itemize}

$\leadsto$ These \textit{early} metrics are not necessarily reliable, but might be useful to identify promising regions and discard bad-performing candidates early.
$\leadsto$ Optimization methods that make use of these are called multi-fidelity methods.

\end{frame}
%-----------------------------------------------------------------------
%----------------------------------------------------------------------
\begin{frame}[c]{Formal definition}

We refer to an inexpensive approximation of the true objective $f(\lambda)$ as $f_b(\lambda)$, where $b \in [b_{min}, b_{max}]$ with $c(\lambda,b)$ being its associated cost. Furthermore, we assume that \item $f_{b_{max}}(\lambda) = f(\lambda)$.

\\~\\
Our optimization objective is then to find:

\begin{equation}
\lambda^* \in \argmin_{\lambda \in \Lambda} f_{b_{max}}(\lambda)
\end{equation}

\end{frame}
%-----------------------------------------------------------------------
%----------------------------------------------------------------------
\begin{frame}[c]{Assumptions}

These methods exploit three structural observations
\begin{enumerate}
    \item the \textbf{error shrinks smoothly} as the fidelity budget increases,
    \item \textbf{ranks are approximately consistent} across fidelities,
    \item and \textbf{higher fidelities provide a more accurate approximation} of the true objective value.
\end{enumerate}

$\leadsto$ in practice, these assumptions are not necessarily satisfied. HPO must be robust against that.

\end{frame}
